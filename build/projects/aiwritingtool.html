Promoting End-to-End Intentionality with Large Language Models

Introduction

Large Language Models, such as ChatGPT or Claude.ai, are applied to many writing tasks and seem like a “magic tool” due to advanced reasoning capabilities, but there isn’t much variety in the interfaces used to request their outputs.

The typical “chat” interface often provides a single, seemingly final answer, which can make users overestimate LLM capabilities and assume its answer is the “best” answer. But we don’t know what really “goes into” an LLM’s training, so we want to help users become more aware of possible limitations.

Approaches

We developed a Microsoft Word sidebar that offered next-sentence suggestions expressed in four different ways:

  • Completion of a current thought: Provides a pre-written single sentence
  • A question about current/future writing: A question the writer could answer in the next sentence
  • A list of possible vocabulary: to use in the next sentence
  • A rhetorical strategy to employ: Directions on the kind of sentence to write, like citing statistics, offering a personal anecdote, etc.

Summary

In a pilot study (N=9), writers found questions and rhetorical moves to be useful and friendly. Multiple participants found that the suggestions helped overcome “writer’s block” and characterized the tool as conversational or collaborative. Although writers chose to request "Completion" more often, they often rejected the suggested text. The majority of participants used the “Completions” option most often, and expressed reservation on how their usage of “Completions” affected their authorship and ownership of the text, but did not raise similar objections to other suggestion types. Dissonance between conscious desire for originality and subconscious desire to minimize cognitive load.

Overall, non-Completion type suggestions seem to hold the most promise in assisting—but not replacing—users’ writing; completions did help with writer’s block when it was aligned with what users wanted to say, but also sometimes induced writers’ block when suggesting ideas that weren’t aligned. This demonstrates the potential for more open-ended suggestions to fill this gap.

Multiple participants rejected more at the beginning then noticed the LLM got a better sense of what they were going for later on, suggesting they were cognitively engaged in evaluating the appropriateness of LLM outputs.
